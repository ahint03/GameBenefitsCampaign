Are Video Games Actually a Waste of Time?

Looking at how games can support learning, stress, and development

The Concern

Video games are often treated as distractions, especially when it comes to younger players. It’s common to worry about screen time, school performance, and whether games are replacing more productive activities.

This is especially relevant for people making decisions about how younger students spend their time, since gaming is often seen as competing with learning.

A Different Way to Look at It

While those concerns aren’t completely wrong, they don’t tell the full story. Instead of only asking whether video games are harmful, it might be more useful to ask what players are actually learning from them.

What Research Shows

Video games can improve problem-solving, motivation, and even help manage stress when used in moderation.

Many games require players to think critically, adapt, and learn through trial and error. This kind of structure is similar to how real-world skills are developed. Research has shown that games can increase motivation and feelings of competence, which are important for both learning and confidence.

Games can also help with stress. Because they require active focus, players often become immersed, giving them a break from outside pressure and allowing them to reset mentally.

Why This Matters

Seeing video games only as distractions ignores the ways they can support development. When used intentionally, they can build persistence, problem-solving skills, and even emotional resilience.

This doesn’t mean gaming should be unlimited, but it does suggest the conversation should shift from restriction to understanding how games are being used.

Rethinking the Role of Games

Video games are a major part of how younger generations interact with media. Because of that, they should be considered as tools that can support both learning and well-being when used intentionally.
